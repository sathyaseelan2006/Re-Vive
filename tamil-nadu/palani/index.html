Palani

The Sacred Abode of Lord Murugan

Discover Palani, one of the six sacred abodes (Arupadaiveedu) of Lord Murugan. Perched atop the sacred Palani Hills, the temple houses the miraculous Navapashanam idol of Lord Dandayudhapani. This revered pilgrimage site attracts millions of devotees annually who climb 659 steps seeking divine blessings and spiritual fulfillment.

One of Six Sacred Abodes

Arupadaiveedu of Lord Murugan

Site Overview

The Palani Murugan Temple, situated atop the sacred Palani Hills at an elevation of 1,500 feet, is one of the most venerated shrines dedicated to Lord Murugan in Tamil Nadu. As one of the six abodes (Arupadaiveedu) of Murugan, this temple holds immense spiritual significance in Tamil culture. The presiding deity, Lord Dandayudhapani, is depicted holding a staff (danda) symbolizing renunciation. The temple's unique Navapashanam idol, crafted from nine herbs and minerals by sage Bogar, is believed to possess miraculous healing properties and draws millions of pilgrims annually.

Urban Legends

The Divine Fruit Contest

According to legend, sage Narada gave a divine fruit (Gnana Palam) to Lord Shiva and Parvati, who decided to gift it to the son who circled the world first. While Murugan set out on his peacock, Ganesha simply circled his parents, stating they were his world. When Murugan returned and found the fruit given to Ganesha, he renounced worldly possessions and settled in Palani as Dandayudhapani, holding only a staff.

Bogar's Alchemical Marvel

The Chinese-Tamil siddha Bogar is said to have created the Navapashanam idol using nine rare herbs and minerals. Legend states he achieved this through ancient alchemical processes that transformed poisonous substances into a healing compound. Many believe the idol still emanates healing vibrations and devotees who pray before it are cured of ailments.

The Undying Flame

Local lore speaks of a sacred eternal flame that burns in the temple's inner sanctum, said to have been lit by Lord Murugan himself when he first arrived at Palani. Temple priests maintain this flame continuously, and it is believed that the flame has never been extinguished in over a thousand years, symbolizing the eternal presence of the deity.

Local Culture

Cuisine

Palani is famous for its sacred Panchamritham prasadam, a delectable mixture of banana, jaggery, ghee, honey, and cardamom. The town also offers traditional South Indian vegetarian fare including dosai, idli, and special temple meals. Local sweets and the temple's holy ash (vibhuti) are cherished by devotees.

Art Forms

The region is renowned for its Kavadi Attam, a devotional dance performed while carrying ornate structures. Traditional Tamil music, especially devotional songs dedicated to Lord Murugan, echoes through the hills. The temple maintains ancient musical traditions with nadaswaram and tavil performances during daily rituals.

Traditions

Devotees undertake tonsuring ceremonies as offerings to Lord Murugan. The practice of carrying Kavadi (ornate burden) up the 659 steps is a significant devotional tradition. Pilgrims often observe fasting and perform 108 circlings of the sanctum as acts of penance and devotion.

Festivals

Major celebrations include Thaipusam, Panguni Uthiram, and the grand Panguni Festival. During Thai Poosam, millions gather as devotees carry milk pots and kavadis in procession. The six-day Panguni Festival features elaborate rituals, chariot processions, and cultural performances attracting devotees worldwide.

Historical Timeline

Mythological Era
Divine Origins

According to legend, Lord Murugan chose Palani Hills as his abode after the divine fruit episode, renouncing worldly possessions and becoming Dandayudhapani (the one holding a staff).

7th-8th Century CE
Temple Foundation

The original temple was established during the Pandya dynasty period. Sage Bogar is believed to have created the miraculous Navapashanam idol using ancient alchemical knowledge during this era.

9th-13th Century CE
Chola Period Expansion

Under Chola patronage, the temple underwent significant renovations and expansions. The temple's architectural features were enhanced, and it gained prominence as one of the important Murugan shrines.

16th-18th Century CE
Nayak Contributions

The Nayak rulers and later the Madurai Nayaks made substantial contributions to the temple, adding gopurams, mandapas, and establishing many of the rituals still followed today.

1966 CE
Modern Facilities

A winch railway system was installed to help elderly and differently-abled pilgrims reach the hilltop temple. Later, a rope car facility was added, modernizing access while preserving the sacred atmosphere.
